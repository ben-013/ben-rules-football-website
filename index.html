BEN RULES FOOTBALL

The greatest sport that never was

Fables of the Reconstruction

Ben McDonald · 1st June 2026 · 7 min read

I loved this Premiership season until the 87th minute at Celtic Park on the final Saturday. But it's important to remember that the Premiership's almost never like this.

Before the season ended, my concern over Heart of Midlothian winning the title (for the record, I still wanted them to do it) was that it would paper over the cracks in a Scottish football landscape that has produced no non-Old Firm champion since 1985.

To be clear, I accept that the Championship, League One and League Two are always compelling. But we seem to be alarmingly content for the elite tier of Scottish football (if that isn't too much of an oxymoron) to be a competitive non-event.

We still insist on pairing Celtic and Livingston in the same division. Why? The main point of competitive sport is to find out who's the best. Why pit two teams against each other on a regular basis where one has an astronomically larger budget than the other?

The idea of deploying a North American-style closed, salary-capped league in Scotland provokes pearl-clutching almost as a reflex. But the Scottish Premiership is structurally uncompetitive. Why are we so hostile to ideas that might address that?

For a moment, imagine taking our sixteen biggest clubs, allocating them into geographic divisions, playing their division rivals four times a season, and everyone else twice a season: 36 games. Then have a playoff for the title at the end. Still four Old Firms. Still four Edinburgh & Dundee derbies. But with fifteen annual league opponents instead of eleven.

Northeast: Aberdeen, Dundee, Dundee United & St Johnstone
Forth: Dunfermline Athletic, Falkirk, Heart of Midlothian & Hibernian
Clyde: Celtic, Motherwell, Partick Thistle & Rangers
Southwest: Ayr United, Kilmarnock, Morton & St Mirren

Is it perfect? No. I hate leaving Raith Rovers out, for one. If we expanded to eighteen clubs, they'd be at the front of the queue.

Could you slice it other ways? Yes, you could resurrect a Lanarkshire derby by swapping Partick Thistle out for Airdrieonians or Hamilton Academical. But realistically, Thistle's the biggest club of the three.

Who else would be aggrieved at being left out? Livingston, Ross County and Inverness Caledonian Thistle probably. But one's from a New Town whose residents haven't really warmed to their local team, one's from a settlement whose population is smaller than its stadium capacity, and one's a Frankenstein's monster manufactured in 1994 (could have been a different story if Caledonian had gone it alone).

Scottish football fans hate the idea of no relegation. I'd challenge them to get over that. Relegation is entertaining for the wrong reasons. It draws your attention the same way a car crash does. With division size limited to four, clubs are always looking up. They're always only a maximum of three places away from winning their division, and the local bragging rights that come with it. If they're out of contention, they can start retooling for the following season without the Sword of Damocles hanging over them.

"What about Motherwell and Partick Thistle stuck in a division with those two? They'll never finish higher than third." A salary cap would sort that, so everyone's playing with roughly the same budget, thus making football a sport again.

But sadly this is not going to wash. A salary cap doesn't automatically give all sixteen clubs Celtic's budget. More likely everyone would average out to something akin to Dundee United's budget this season. United fought admirably against Austrian giants, Rapid Wien, in Europe this season - only succumbing in a penalty shoot-out. But perennial elimination before the league phase isn't going to work for all of Scotland's European participants.

The reality is that Scotland can't implement this in isolation. Celtic and Rangers would never accept a salary cap that reduced their ability to compete in Europe, nor would UEFA permit a radically different financial model in one country while the rest of the continent continued as normal. But if we're discussing what would make Scottish football more competitive and entertaining, rather than what is politically achievable tomorrow morning, then a closed, salary-capped league remains a more compelling vision than endlessly arguing over whether the Premiership should have twelve, fourteen or sixteen clubs.

Ditch VAR as well, it's awful.
